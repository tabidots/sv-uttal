In many words, *k* (and to a lesser extent *sk*) contradicts the "Swedish 101" rule by being pronounced hard despite preceding a soft vowel.

One important tendency (based on the morphemic principle mentioned in the introduction) is that a hard *k* at the end of a word does not become soft before the suffixes

- **-er • -else • -eri • -era • -ering**
- **-isk • -ism • -ist • -ig(het)**

## soft *k* in -ker- (2)

Besides *-kera/-kering*, in which *k* is always hard, even *-ker-* as a word fragment is pronounced with a hard *k* so consistently that the two words where it forms part of the root and is pronounced with a soft *k* are the exception:

- **<Mark>k</Mark>eramik** 
- **<Mark>k</Mark>erub**
