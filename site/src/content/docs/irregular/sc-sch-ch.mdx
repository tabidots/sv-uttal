*sc*, *sch*, and *ch* are foreign letter combinations with no defined pronunciation in Swedish, while *stg* is a rare combination with an optional exceptional pronunciation.

<Aside type="note">
It seems that many speakers of Swedish today who do not have a marked regional accent do not differentiate the tj-sound and the rs-sound. They never occur in the same position in a word, so no confusion arises from merging them. For simplicity, these sounds will be referred to here as the sh-sound, even though the letter combination *sh* does not actually occur in native Swedish words.
</Aside>

## *sc* (2)

Dictionary data and anecdotal data on these two uncommon word groups is messy and it is difficult to say anything conclusively. However, it would be safe to pronounce them as follows:

- **la<Mark>sc</Mark>iv, la<Mark>sc</Mark>ivitet** - *"lashiv-"*
- **o<Mark>sc</Mark>illation** (3): o<Mark>sc</Mark>illation, o<Mark>sc</Mark>illering, o<Mark>sc</Mark>illograf - *"osilla-"*

## *stg* (2)

These can optionally be pronounced as written (e.g., *vast•jöte*).

- **vä<Mark>stg</Mark>öte**, **vä<Mark>stg</Mark>ötska** 
- **ö<Mark>stg</Mark>öte**, **ö<Mark>stg</Mark>ötska** 

## *sch*

*sch* is normally pronounced with an sh-sound before consonants (**<Mark>sch</Mark>lager**) or at the end of a syllable (**affi<Mark>sch</Mark>**).

This is also the general case before certain suffixes, even if the suffix starts with a vowel:

- affi<Mark>sch</Mark>•era, affi<Mark>sch</Mark>•ering, affi<Mark>sch</Mark>•pelare, affi<Mark>sch</Mark>•ör
- kly<Mark>sch</Mark>•a, kly<Mark>sch</Mark>•ig
