*g* is probably the most surprising consonant in Swedish. The "Swedish 101" rule about *g* seems to hold true least often compared to the rules for all other letters except *o*. *j* is included in this section because the sj-sound pronunciation is not typical for either letter.

## Ultra-exceptions

The exceptions haven't even been mentioned yet but some here are some important exceptions to those exceptions.

- **sä<Mark>g</Mark>a** (7): avsä<Mark>g</Mark>a, det vill sä<Mark>g</Mark>a, eftersä<Mark>g</Mark>are, försä<Mark>g</Mark>a, frånsä<Mark>g</Mark>a, förutsä<Mark>g</Mark>a, motsä<Mark>g</Mark>a, sä<Mark>g</Mark>a, ja-sägare

In all other words where *g* is pronounced soft before *a*, the softness is triggered by *-rga-* or *-lga-* (i.e., the "Swedish 101" rule takes precedence). Meanwhile, in words where *rg* or *lg* is pronounced hard before a soft vowel, these are listed in the section "hard g before soft vowel" below.

<Aside type="note">
**-sägare** is only exceptional when it is *säga* + *-are* ("sayer"); in compounds with **ägare** (owner), the *g* is pronounced hard, despite the intervening *-s-*.

- **ägare**: bruksägare, butiksägare, fastighetsägare, fordringsägare, godsägare, hemmansägare, husägare, majoritetsägare, minoritetsägare, målsägare, skogsägare
</Aside>

Also, the suffixes *-gera/-gering* usually trigger a hard *g*, except in this word group:

- **re<Mark>g</Mark>era /-ering** (26): re<Mark>g</Mark>era, delstatsre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, exilre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, förmyndarre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, högerre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, koalitionsre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, majoritetsre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, militärre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, minoritetsre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, re<Mark>g</Mark>ering, vänsterre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, övergångsre<Mark>g</Mark>ering, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsbeslut, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringschef, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsförklaring, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsförslag, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringskansli, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsmakt, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsparti, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsråd, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringssammanträde, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsstyrka, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringstalesman, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringstjänsteman, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsuppdrag, re<Mark>g</Mark>eringsår

## Tendencies

Like *k*, a hard *g* at the end of a word does not become soft before the suffixes

- **-er • -else • -eri • -era • -ering**
- **-isk • -ism • -ist • -ig(het) • -isering**

and does not become hard in the adjective endings **-igare / -igaste**.

*g* is always hard in the following fragments when they are not part of a word:

- **-lo<Mark>g</Mark>i-**: from Greek (English *-logy*). *analo<Mark>g</Mark>i*, *ekolo<Mark>g</Mark>i*, *lo<Mark>g</Mark>ik*...
- **-al<Mark>g</Mark>i-**: from Greek (English *-algia*). *fibromyal<Mark>g</Mark>i*, *nostal<Mark>g</Mark>i* ...
- **-fa<Mark>g</Mark>i-**: from Greek (English *-phagy*). *antropofa<Mark>g</Mark>i*, *autofa<Mark>g</Mark>i* ...
- **-ur<Mark>g</Mark>i-**: from Greek (English *-urgy*). *dramatur<Mark>g</Mark>i*, *metallur<Mark>g</Mark>i*...
- **-<Mark>g</Mark>eri-**: this includes **-eri-** as a suffix (English *-ery*), as in *bedrä<Mark>g</Mark>eri*, as well as *-<Mark>g</Mark>eri-* as part of a word, like *Ni<Mark>g</Mark>eria* 

<Aside type="note">
As listed below, **lo<Mark>g</Mark>i** (*accommodation, lodging*) by itself is pronounced with an sj-sound.
</Aside>
