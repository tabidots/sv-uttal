This analysis is the result of examining approximately 3500 distinct verb lexemes in Wiktionary using pronunciation data from the Braxen dataset. For the sake of simplicity, phrasal verbs were excluded (the rationale is similar to that for excluding compound nouns in the noun analysis.)

## General pattern

In terms of pitch accent, there are essentially only four kinds of verbs, as determined by the pitch accents of the infinitive and the present tense.

| infinitve | present | type |
| --- | --- | --- |
| any | monosyllabic | "Hidden" grave accent |
| grave | acute | Shifting accent | 
| grave | grave | Stable grave accent |
| acute | acute | Stable acute accent |

However, when divided by traditional verb group (*-er* verbs, *-ar* verbs and short verbs), some patterns become immediately clear.

### Stable acute accent

Given the above tendencies, this category includes all verbs beginning with *fﾃｶr-* (but not *fﾃｶre-* or *fﾃｶr窶｢be-*) or *be-*.

It also includes **all verbs** ending in *-erar* or *-eras*, which is quite a large group.

There are just a few verbs with a stable acute accent that do not fit into either of the above groups. Most, like *designar*, are derived from foreign loanwords, except for *anammar, valackar, vﾃ､lsignar.*

### Stable grave accent

All other prefixed verbs fall into this category. As shown in the examples earlier, the prefix steals the stress from the infinitive ending. This leads to two stresses in stable positions throughout all inflections.
