This analysis is the result of examining 8186 distinct non-compound noun lexemes in Wiktionary using pronunciation data from the Braxen dataset. For the sake of simplicity, the following were excluded:

- compound nouns
- nouns that did not have a plural form
- most lexemes that included one or more ambiguous wordforms

<Aside type="note">
The definite plural will always have the same accent as the indefinite plural, so there is no column for it in the following tables.
</Aside>

## Tendencies

### Polysyllabic nouns

In the majority of cases, Swedish nouns are **polysyllabic** (the *dictionary form* has more than one syllable) and have a **stable** pitch accent across all forms, regardless of whether it is an acute or grave accent.

6310/8003 (78.9%) of all nouns and 6310/6448 (97.8%) of polysyllabic nouns fit this description. 1123/6309 (17.8%) of these stable-accent nouns are *null-plural* nouns (the indefinite singular and plural forms are identical).

### Monosyllabic nouns

Meanwhile, 1057/1641 (64.9%) of **monosyllabic** nouns (where the *dictionary form* has one syllable) exhibit the following pattern: The primary stressed syllable remains the same throughout, but the plural forms **shift** to a grave accent, giving the impression of an "added" secondary stress.

By definition, this will never occur in null-plural monosyllablic nouns.

## Exceptions

### Polysyllabic nouns: acute to grave

Fewer than 3% of polysyllabic nouns have a pitch accent pattern that **shifts** between singular and plural forms. The largest group of these is made up of nouns whose accent shifts from acute to grave (giving the impression of an "added" secondary stress). Notably, all of the days of the week are in this group. The remainder appear to be mostly nouns ending in unstressed *-er*, *-en*, *-el* in their dictionary form.
